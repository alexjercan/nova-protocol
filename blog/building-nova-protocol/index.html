Building Nova Protocol: modular ships and a diegetic autopilot

Nova Protocol started as a pile of Bevy examples and grew into a build-and-fly space shooter. Two decisions shaped almost everything else: ships are assemblies of small sections, and the flight computer flies those ships for real instead of scripting their position.

Ships are bags of sections

A ship in Nova Protocol is not a monolithic model - it is a root entity with a handful of section children: hull, controller, thruster, turret, torpedo bay. Each section carries its own mass and health and contributes exactly one behavior. The controller steers, the thrusters push, the turrets aim and shoot.

The nice property falls out for free: damage is local. Blow the turret off a hull and the ship keeps flying but stops shooting; take out the controller and it can no longer steer itself. Nothing special-cases this - it is just what happens when the section that owned a capability is gone.

The autopilot flies the hull

The part that makes the game feel like a spaceship sim is the diegetic autopilot. When you press GOTO, ORBIT or STOP, the flight computer does not slide the ship to a destination - it drives the same controller and thrusters you would, so you watch the hull physically swing to a new heading and the exhaust plume light up as it burns.

  • GOTO burns toward your current lock, flips at the arrival curve, and decelerates to rest at a standoff distance.
  • ORBIT parks you into a stable circular orbit around the dominant gravity well.
  • STOP faces retrograde and burns until you are at rest.

Because it flies through the real actuators, it is honest about the ship's limits - a sluggish, under-thrustered build takes longer to come around than a nimble one. And the moment you touch a control, the autopilot disengages and hands you back a ship that is already moving.

Gravity that means something

Large asteroids carry gravity wells with real inverse-square pull, clamped near the surface and faded to nothing at the edge of their sphere of influence. That is what ORBIT flies around, and it is the single mechanic that turns "fly to a point" into "manage your orbit". The main menu even shows it off: the ship drifting behind the panel is an AI flying a live ORBIT maneuver, not a looping animation.

What is next

More scenarios, deeper objectives, and a real settings screen. Follow along in the repository, or just go fly something.
