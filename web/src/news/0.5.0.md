# v0.5.0 - Lock, look, and a front door

v0.4.0 gave Nova Protocol combat, an AI to fight, and a lot of automatic
targeting. v0.5.0 takes that targeting and hands the controls back to the player,
deliberately: passive auto-acquisition is gone, replaced by hold-to-lock radar, a
corner viewfinder that renders your target through a second camera, and a kill cam
when it dies. It also does the thing every project eventually has to do - it teaches
a new player how to fly through the Shakedown Run, gives damage a typed grain, boots
into a real main menu, and puts up a front door on the web. Here is the whole of it,
down to the nuts and crannies.

## Locking is a decision now

The biggest change in feel: all the passive auto-targeting from the last release is
gone, replaced by deliberate **radar locking**. Hold CTRL to sweep and live-lock
whatever you look at; your stance picks the slot - lowered gives a white NAV
crosshair that feeds GOTO, raised gives a red combat reticle that feeds guns,
torpedoes and fine-lock. Tapping CTRL clears the lock in stages - combat first, then
nav - and a lock sticks until the target dies, leaves range, or goes cold.
Crucially, LOCK is now framed as a ship-computer capability, the same way GOTO is:
it is something the ship does for you, not a magic HUD overlay.

### Stances pick the slot

The stance-to-slot mapping is the heart of it. With weapons lowered, the sweep
paints a white NAV crosshair, and that lock is the one GOTO and the autopilot
consume. Raise weapons and the same gesture paints a red combat reticle instead,
feeding the guns, the torpedo solution, and the fine-lock that picks out a single
section. Because the two locks live in different slots, a nav lock and a combat lock
can coexist: you can be flying toward one body while your guns track another.

### Clearing in stages

Clearing is deliberate too. A tap of CTRL drops the combat lock first, and a second
tap drops the nav lock, so you never lose your navigation target just because you
wanted to stop shooting. Short of a manual clear, a lock holds on its own until one
of three things ends it - the target dies, it drifts out of range, or it goes cold
and stops returning a signal.

### Slot-colored lock language

The lock language got slot-colored to match: a red bracket is a combat lock, white
is a nav lock, and the old relation tints and reticle pips were retired outright, so
color now means one thing only. Turrets hold the combat lock even while you aim them
manually, so peeling off to hand-fire a turret does not throw away the lock the rest
of the ship is using.

## The target viewfinder and kill cam

To make a lock feel like something you have, v0.5.0 adds a **target viewfinder**: a
corner inset that renders a live, magnified 3D view of your combat lock through a
second camera. It shows a red armed frame while your weapons are hot, a NO-SIGNAL
panel for bodies that cannot be scoped, and - the favorite touch - a roughly
two-second freeze-frame **kill cam** when the target dies. The fine-locked section
glows in both the main view and the inset, so you always know exactly what you are
shooting at.

### What the inset will and will not scope

The inset is picky by design. It zooms combat bodies only - ships, torpedoes,
asteroids - and pointedly not beacons, which carry no combat identity worth
scoping. For bodies that have no addressable sections, it falls back to framing them
by their collider bounds, so a section-less rock still fills the inset cleanly
instead of sitting tiny in a corner. Anything the second camera cannot resolve gets
the NO-SIGNAL panel rather than a broken render.

## The Shakedown Run

New Game now starts the **Shakedown Run**, a roughly twelve-beat tutorial that walks
a new pilot through the whole vocabulary: burn, freelook, stop, salvage, GOTO,
coasting through a gravity well, ORBIT, radar lock, a live-fire rehearsal on a
derelict, and finally a real scavenger fight. Each beat teaches exactly one gesture
and completes the instant that gesture lands, so it never blocks a player who already
knows the moves.

### One gesture per beat

The pacing is strict: burn to learn the throttle, freelook to learn the camera, stop
to learn the brake, salvage to learn the loop, GOTO to hand a target to the
autopilot, a gravity-well coast to feel orbital mechanics, ORBIT to hold a ring,
radar lock to acquire, a live-fire rehearsal on a derelict to safely pull the
trigger, and only then a real scavenger fight to put it all together. Because every
beat completes on the gesture itself rather than on a timer, a returning pilot can
blow through the whole run without ever being stalled.

### The playtest tail

Making it playable shook out a long tail of playtest fixes. Park points were sized
to sit inside their beacon triggers so arriving actually counts; orbit-hold now
completes when the ring is held rather than merely touched; scavenger spawn timing
was tuned and paired with a 150u combat leash so the fight stays where it was
staged. Planetoids were made invulnerable so a stray burst cannot brick a beat, the
speed cap gets taught rather than silently enforced, and the objective text got
readable wrapping and better sound pacing with legible gold pulses. Crucially,
asteroid kills now emit OnDestroyed under the scenario id, which had soft-locked the
derelict beat when the script never heard the kill it was waiting on.

## Damage with a grain

Combat picked up some texture too. Damage is now **typed** - Kinetic, AP, EMP,
Explosive - and checked against per-section resistance tables, so what you shoot
matters as much as where you shoot it.

### Typed damage and resistance tables

Every hit now carries one of four types - Kinetic, AP, EMP, or Explosive - and
each section carries its own resistance table against those four types, so the same
round can be devastating against one section and shrugged off by the next. Picking
the right ammunition for the section you are aiming at is now a real decision rather
than flavor.

### Ammo slots

Each turret carries a loaded-ammo slot that sets the type of the rounds it fires,
surfaced through a color-coded ammo readout so you can see at a glance what is
loaded. The turret's slot and the target's resistance table are the two halves of
the same equation.

### PDC retuned to point defense

The point-defense cannon was retuned to actually be point defense: per-hit damage
dropped from 20 to 4, so the stream chips a target down over a visible burst instead
of deleting it in a single frame. That turns the PDC back into a suppression weapon
you watch work, rather than an instant-delete button.

### Sensor projectiles that curve

Turret bullets are now sensor projectiles - same damage, no physical shove, expended
on first contact - so a burst no longer knocks targets around like a battering ram,
and each round spends itself the moment it connects. Those rounds now curve through
gravity wells like ships and torpedoes, so shots near a planetoid arc the way
everything else in the world does instead of flying dead straight through a field
that bends every other body.

## A game that boots into a menu

v0.5.0 is the release where Nova Protocol starts to feel like a shipped game rather
than a scene loader. It boots into a **main menu** with a live ambient backdrop - an
AI ship flying a thruster-driven orbit - plus an ESC pause menu, all living in a new
`nova_menu` crate. Booting into the menu, rather than dropping straight into a scene,
is the small structural change that makes the whole thing read as a game with a
front door.

### Diegetic flight readouts

The flight readouts went diegetic, replacing the old bottom-left status text. Speed
and engaged-mode chips now sit beside the velocity sphere where your eyes already
are, ORBIT draws a radius spoke so the hold ring is a thing you can see, and the
whole cluster takes on a nav-cyan tint while the autopilot is flying so you always
know when the ship has the stick.

### HUD visibility levels

The HUD learned **visibility levels**: grave/tilde cycles ALL, then MINIMAL, then
NONE, so you can strip the interface down to a clean view for a screenshot or a quiet
cruise and cycle it right back.

### Contextual keybinds

The keybind cluster is now contextual: a verb's row appears only while that verb can
actually act, so the prompts on screen are always things you can do right now.
Scenario-emphasized keys show up early and pulse gold to draw the eye to whatever the
current beat wants from you.

## Objectives and the scenario system

Objectives got easier to follow, and the scenario system underneath grew the
primitives that made the Shakedown Run expressible as pure data.

### Objective conveyance

A gold marker chip now carries live distance to the current target so you always
know how far you have to go. Salvage crates glow and wear brackets so they read as
things to grab, keybind emphasis pulses point you at the next gesture, and finishing
a beat lands with a completion chime and a posting blip so success is unmistakable.

### New scenario primitives

Underneath, the scenario system gained new primitives: nav beacons and salvage
crates with authorable radar signatures, so a scenario can place a body and specify
exactly how it reads on the radar; despawn-by-id, so a script can remove a specific
body it spawned; and a batch of new events and actions - OnOrbit, OnTravelLock and
OnCombatLock to react to the player acquiring or holding a target, SetSpeedCap to
teach the throttle limit, plus objective markers and hint emphasis. Together these
are what let the twelve-beat run be authored as data rather than hand-wired code.

## Ships and the editor

### Rebindable section keys

In the editor, placed sections now show their bound key as a chip, and you can click
that chip to rebind the section to a different key or mouse button. The build panel
scrolls too, so a busy ship no longer runs its section list off the bottom of the
screen.

### A passive play-test target

The editor's play-test ship is now a passive target instead of an AI combatant, so
you can shoot at your own build to check weapon coverage without the target shooting
back.

## A home on the web

Finally, the thing you are reading this on. v0.5.0 ships a **web landing site** (the
`web/` directory: TypeScript, Webpack and Tailwind), and the GitHub Pages deploy now
serves it at the root with the playable game tucked under `/play/`. This news feed
and the game's new web home start here, with the wiki and tutorial following shortly
after in v0.5.2.

## Under the hood

A handful of quieter fixes rounded the release out. The debug inspector stays on a
window camera instead of leaking into render-to-texture cameras, so it no longer
bleeds into the viewfinder's second-camera render. HUD apparent-size elements -
reticles and brackets - now measure real colliders rather than the invisible trigger
volumes around them, so their on-screen size matches the body they wrap. Debug builds
no longer crash when a scenario advances, since the smoke assertion now covers only
the boot load, and teardown no longer warns about despawned entities. And two bits of
tidy-up: F1 back-to-editor is sandbox-only so it cannot fire from a real run, and the
debug ammo readout no longer lingers on screen once debug mode is off.

## Point releases

Two patches followed close behind, and between them they cleared the web-build
crashes and turned the site into a place you can actually learn from.

### v0.5.1 - Web build shakeout

Shortly after 0.5.0 shipped, **v0.5.1** cleaned up two crashes unique to the shipped
WASM build. Both only bit the browser when you started a run - New Game from the menu
or Play from the editor - and never touched native, which is why they slipped past
into the shipped app. The target inset was carrying a `view_formats` override that
WebGL2 does not support for a plain sRGB target, and the render validation error it
raised was fatal, so the inset now drops that override entirely.

The second was subtler. The skybox cubemap's `.meta` loader settings had silently
stopped applying in the shipped app: `AssetMetaCheck::Never` ignored them without a
word, which resurrected an oversized-upload race that crashes GPUs with a 16384
texture limit. With the meta settings honored again, the cubemap loads within the
limit and the race is gone. If you play on the web, this is the one to grab.

### v0.5.2 - Learn the ropes on the web

A little later, **v0.5.2** turned the site into a place you can actually learn from.

A full **wiki** landed - gameplay, ship sections, keybinds, world, and meta pages -
so the game's systems finally have a written home, alongside a tutorial trimmed down
to first-scenario onboarding and two new devlogs. That trim was deliberate: the
tutorial now covers exactly the first scenario rather than trying to document
everything at once.

Gamepad bindings got rounded out. ORBIT now maps to South, scenario-advance confirm
to DPadDown, and HUD cycle, pause, and back-to-editor each gained a button, so a
controller can drive the game and its menus end to end.

The examples became a testable curriculum: twelve numbered examples, from controller
PD through the full boot flow, each self-driving under `BCS_AUTOPILOT` with behavior
assertions and a completion backstop. CI's example smoke suite is BLOCKING again now
that the GitHub-runner-only taffy panic is gone, so those twelve examples gate every
push. The smoke run now fails on ANY logged command error, closing the gap where
handled remove/despawn warnings - the stale-entity teardown race - used to sail past
the panic gate. And the weapon test ranges fire again: the weapons safety had
silently disarmed both, so their scripts now raise the stance first, with new
assertions pinning the fire-to-hit chain.

A handful of audio and teardown bugs got fixed too. Thruster hum now attenuates with
distance per ship, so another ship's or a torpedo's burn no longer plays at full
volume from anywhere in the map, while your own ship stays exempt. Scenario teardown
no longer logs an "Entity despawned" command error when a ship despawns with
autopilot engaged: the telemetry-cleanup observer now uses the fallible remove
variant, pinned by regression tests. And the debug inspector's window-camera
placement fix moved upstream into bevy-common-systems (rev 4a743b2), deleting nova's
local workaround.
