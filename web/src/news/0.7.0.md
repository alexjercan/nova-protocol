# v0.7.0 - A game you can win or lose

For six releases Nova Protocol was a place to fly, not a game you could finish.
There was no win, no loss, no reason a fight had to end - you flew until you got
bored or blown up, and blown up just dropped you back at a silent restart.
v0.7.0 is the release that gives the flying stakes: scenarios can now declare a
**victory or a defeat**, the base campaign grew a real second chapter that
chains off the first, a full four-chapter **campaign mod** ships on the portal,
and the fights themselves got smarter - enemies that take cover, weapons that
reload, locks you have to earn. Around that there is a real Settings menu, fine
docking thrusters, and a pile of authoring and content tooling. Here is the
whole of it, section by section.

## Win or lose

The headline is the smallest-sounding change: scenarios can now end. A new
`Outcome` action throws up a **VICTORY** or **DEFEAT** overlay with real buttons
- Continue or Retry, and Main Menu always - instead of the old silent
press-Enter-to-restart. Dying in the Shakedown Run now shows Defeat and offers
Retry; clearing it shows Victory and chains you straight into chapter two.

The part that took the most care is what the overlay does to the world behind it.
It **freezes the simulation** the same way the pause menu does - physics, AI,
weapons and scenario timers all stop dead behind the banner - so the world does
not quietly play on underneath a screen you are reading. The closing story lines
that used to fire behind the victory banner (where nobody could ever read them)
now live inside the banner itself.

## Broadside, and a campaign that talks back

**Broadside** is chapter two of the base storyline and the first place the win/lose
frame really earns its keep: answer a neutral hauler's distress call, break a
two-corvette ambush, then screen a gang gunship's torpedoes with your
point-defense and pull it apart. It is the capital-combat slice, it is in the
Scenarios picker, and it is chained from Shakedown Run's victory screen so the
base game now plays as a sequence, not a menu of disconnected scenes. It also
picked up a **checkpoint**: break the ambush and a Victory beat banks your
progress, so dying to the gunship retries the gunship rather than marching you
back through the whole approach.

The whole campaign got a **storytelling pass** at the same time. Scenarios can
now speak: a new `StoryMessage` action shows speaker-attributed dialog in a HUD
comms panel (`OKONO > Strip it clean.`), the comms lines queue and display in
arrival order instead of a burst overwriting its own first line, and each fades
in, holds for a readable dwell, and yields to the next. Every fight now
announces itself, attackers fly in on a readable **arrival grace** before going
hot instead of materializing point-blank, and the opening comms beats are spaced
on the scenario clock rather than dumped all at once. The beat-sheet convention
is written down for scenario authors, and the content lint enforces its two hard
rules.

## The Ledger: a full campaign on the portal

If Broadside proves the base game can tell a story, **The Ledger** proves a mod
can. It is the first campaign mod on the portal: a four-chapter salvage arc -
strip the wreck, break the claim jumpers, run the quiet channel, then pick the
buyer, or the buoy - with comms-driven story beats and a **two-ending finale**,
all authored in the RON scenario stack v0.6.0 shipped. Install it from Mods >
Explore.

Dogfooding the authoring stack on a real campaign is also what surfaced the
balance work. The Ledger's chapter two was rebuilt around fair fights: the
opening wave carries a light mook gun instead of the top-tier turret, burns in
from a single lane instead of flanking you point-blank, and an invulnerable
boulder field gives the new cover rules something to work with. Breaking wave one
is a checkpoint, so dying to the reinforced heavies retries the heavies, not the
whole chapter. The finale's Auditor traded its top-tier turret for the mook gun
on both ending branches - its drama is the close entrance and the torpedo you
screen, not an instant shred.

## Smarter fights

The combat model grew four teeth this cycle.

**Enemies take cover.** A gunner with an asteroid - or any other tangible body -
between its muzzle and you now **holds fire** instead of hosing the rock, and
holds torpedo launches the same way. Its attack orbit keeps it circling, so the
pressure resumes once the angle comes back, but breaking line of sight behind
hard cover now actually relieves you instead of just soaking bullets. To give
the rule something to bite on, the big fights gained a handful of **invulnerable
boulders** as real cover, with the destructible rock field kept as chaff.

**Weapons reload.** A magazine that runs dry now refills on its own instead of
leaving the weapon dead for the rest of the scenario - PDCs dump and reload to
full after a few seconds, the torpedo bay rearms one tube at a time. Because a
spent weapon always comes back, magazine size is a **fire-pacing beat** rather
than a hard fail, so finite ammo is on by default now: the campaign flies with
real ammo and the diegetic ammo readout, and the gauge fills back up as a reload
sweep so you can read how close a weapon is to firing again.

**Locks are earned.** Locking is no longer instant. Once the radar settles on a
target you hold it under your aim through a short **lock-on dwell** before it
commits, and the dwell is longer the farther the target is. Sweep off before it
fills and the acquisition cancels. A lock is now something you keep the target
under your aim to earn, not something granted the instant you point at it.

**Turrets can carry more than one barrel.** A mount now fires and aims every
muzzle it has, so a twin-barrel PDC throws two streams at once, each at its own
fire rate, all sharing the turret's one magazine - so a twin barrel empties it
twice as fast.

## Docking on the fine thrusters

New this cycle: a **reaction-control system** for the close-in work autopilot and
main-drive burns are too coarse for. Hold **SHIFT** and steer with the mouse
(lateral and fore/aft) and the scroll wheel (up and down) to nudge the ship along
its own axes under a gentle speed cap, with no rotation - the helm and camera
hold still while you translate, the velocity sphere turns violet, and a soft burn
loop tells you it is live.

It is deliberately a **trim, not free thrust**: each axis caps around 2 u/s, so
RCS is for easing the last few meters of an approach, not for flying. The
autopilot uses the same thrusters under the hood - GOTO and STOP now settle their
arrival on RCS, braking with the fine jets in the last stretch so a ship eases to
a stop instead of pulsing on the spot. RCS is a controller verb granted per ship,
and the mainline campaign flies with it withheld (the `[SHIFT] RCS` hint only
shows when a scenario grants it), so it stays out of the way until a scenario
that wants docking opts a ship in.

## A real Settings menu

The Settings menu is real content now instead of a placeholder. It carries a
draggable master audio volume slider, a **Low / Medium / High graphics-quality
preset**, and a read-only reference of the current keyboard and gamepad bindings
(flight, targeting, camera, pause). It is reachable from both the main menu and
the pause menu - the same modal, both doors - and it is remembered across
restarts, as a config file on native and browser storage on the web.

The graphics preset is not just cosmetic. High keeps the full combat juice
(camera shake, hit flashes); Medium drops the shake; Low turns the juice off
entirely and also gates the heavier visuals for weak machines - it is spawn-less
(no particle bursts) and renders the world at a reduced internal resolution that
upscales to the window. That last lever is aimed squarely at fill-bound hardware
(laptop iGPUs, phones); the HUD and menus stay crisp and fully clickable, and on
a strong discrete GPU the win is small - it is a knob for the low end, not a
general speed-up.

## Turrets, art and time: the modding surface

The authoring stack grew in three big ways this cycle, each a format change worth
reading if you write mods.

**Turret mounts are an arbitrary joint tree.** A `Turret` section now describes
its gun as a `root` joint with recursive `children` - each joint an offset, an
optional hinge axis the aim solver steers, an optional render mesh, and an
optional muzzle fire point - instead of the old fixed yaw/pitch/barrel fields. A
mount can have extra hinges, a rotator chain, or elevation several joints down,
and the aim solver distributes the lead across whatever hinges a muzzle hangs
below, so novel mounts track without bespoke code. The stock turrets aim and fire
exactly as before.

**Mods can ship their own art and sounds.** A bundle manifest gains a `resources`
list of the binary files it packages, and content references them with a
`self://` path that resolves against the mod's own folder. Cross-mod and
base-game references got one canonical form too: `self://` for your own folder,
`dep://<id>/` for a declared dependency's file, and `dep://base/` for a
base-game asset. The base game itself became a normal self-contained mod - its
art moved under `assets/base/` and is referenced like any dependency. Every world
sound is now content-owned the same way: a turret's fire, a controller's radar
ticks, a section's impact and destruction voice, a thruster's hum - all authorable
asset refs, so a modded gun sounds like its own gun.

**Scenarios can tell time.** The engine now maintains a reserved
`scenario_elapsed` variable - seconds of live, unpaused scenario time, frozen by
pause and reset by retry - readable from any expression filter, so authors can
write timed story beats, breathers and late reinforcement waves with the existing
gate vocabulary. The `example` mod ships a timed comms nudge and a timed bonus
spawn as the copy-me pattern.

## Tooling that catches your mistakes

The content tools are one CLI now: `cargo run -p nova_assets --bin content --
<gen|lint|audit>` replaces the three former separate binaries. Behind it, the
checks got much stricter this cycle, because building two campaigns worth of
content surfaced whole classes of authoring slip:

- **Geometry checks.** The lint now errors on ships whose unit-cube sections
  overlap, and on a turret or torpedo bay whose base face does not seat against
  an occupied neighbor cell - the exact bugs behind the Auditor's embedded
  torpedo bay and the Rust Tally's four wrong-rolled side mounts.
- **A balance audit.** `content -- audit` derives each combat scenario's fairness
  sheet from the shipped content (hostile dps, weapon envelopes, spawn distances,
  time-to-kill, cover tiers) and fails CI when an armed hostile opens a scenario
  already inside its own effective range of the player spawn. It earned its keep
  on the first run by flagging the Auditor. Intended drama can be acknowledged in
  `balance_acks.ron` with a reason and the deciding task; a stale ack fails CI
  until pruned.
- **Broken content fails loud.** Scenarios with reference errors refuse to start
  with a FAILED TO START report naming each problem, and the menu backdrop
  rotation skips them rather than crashing.

## Odds and ends

- **Two new menu backdrops** join the rotation behind the menus: Waystation
  Traffic (a hauler convoy circling a freight stop under amber dock lights) and
  Scrapyard Drift (a quiet salvage yard of drifting crates, two wrecks and a lone
  tug). Mods can ship their own, too - any scenario flagged `menu_backdrop` is
  eligible, and the game picks one at random on each menu entry.
- **One `example` mod** now folds the earlier scattered demos into a single
  copy-me tutorial: a section overlay, a brand-new section, a playable arena, its
  own shipped skybox and texture, a comms beat, Victory/Defeat outcomes and a menu
  backdrop - a little of everything, in one folder.
- The **diegetic ammo gauge** shows reload progress; the **pause menu** gained a
  Retry button; a ship that loses its last section no longer lingers as a
  targetable ghost hull; restarting a scenario no longer blanks the objectives
  panel; and the deep-field skybox crash on WebGL2-class GPUs is fixed for base
  and mod cubemaps alike.
